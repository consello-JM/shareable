02How We Partner

The engagement is two layers, designed to reinforce each other. We deliver execution capacity where we have direct operating expertise — and we sit alongside the broader portfolio as an advisory overlay across everything else.

Layer One · Execution

Where we do the work

Direct operating support on the workstreams where we have lived experience and can move from day one. Small teams — three to five people — embedded shoulder-to-shoulder with internal leaders.

  • Membership architecture and loyalty economics
  • Marketing operating-model and agency-restructure
  • Brand narrative integration and investor-day storytelling
  • First-party data activation and commercial productization
Layer Two · Advisory Overlay

Where we sit alongside

An ongoing thought-partnership across areas we are not staffing directly — including the parts of the business that are working well. The overlay exists to bring independent inputs to the decisions that matter most.

  • Pressure-testing priorities — separating urgent from important
  • Sharpening objectives so wins are measurable and defensible
  • Independent inputs from outside the operating cadence
  • Pattern recognition from operators who have been in the seat
Every recommendation in this guide is screened against three filters — Impact, Relevance, Feasibility.
The screen is shown explicitly on each area below.

01
Lens — Whiteside / Grossman Confidence 85%

Stand up a Blue Buffalo / fresh pet membership before The Farmer’s Dog defines the category.

Why this leads

Pet operations are well-run and don’t need outside help. But the membership and subscription architecture decision sitting on top of Pet is a different question — and it is the most Consello-shaped problem on the list. Janey built Walmart+ from inside Walmart. Mindy ran HSN’s membership-driven omnichannel. The architecture window in pet humanization closes fast, and the structural call made now compounds for a decade.

The strategic frame

Pet is the bright spot — Harmening has said so on three consecutive earnings calls. Pet humanization continues to outperform the rest of the portfolio. Blue Buffalo’s Buddies app is live, and General Mills has publicly committed to fresh pet food investment to compete with The Farmer’s Dog (FY26 commentary).

The Farmer’s Dog reportedly does $1B+ in annual revenue on a subscription model with no scaled physical retail presence. Blue Buffalo has every input The Farmer’s Dog has — brand, retail distribution, formulation expertise — plus assets they don’t. The strategic question is not whether to compete; it is how the architecture lines up. Specifically: do Buddies and the new fresh business report as one membership product or two channels? That is a structural call. Structural calls compound.

What execution looks like
  • Membership tier design: price points, benefit ladder, tier triggers, what crosses the paywall
  • Buddies / fresh integration architecture: single ID, unified P&L, shared CRM, joint pricing logic
  • Disclosure design: which subscriber metrics start reporting, when, how — built in coordination with IR before launch, not after
  • Brand permission work: positioning Blue Buffalo as a premium retail brand with subscription extension, not a subscription-first DTC competitor
  • Operator partnership: we work alongside the internal Pet team and existing supply-chain leaders — we do not own cold-chain, fulfillment, or formulation
What lands at the next investor day
  • Launched membership tier with stated subscriber count and retention metric
  • Pet segment disclosure that gives the Street a recurring-revenue line to underwrite
  • Multiple expansion potential — Wall Street pays for visible recurring revenue in a way it does not pay for shelf-stable kibble
Impact
High
First disclosable subscriber metric in Pet. Re-rates how the Street values the segment. Defines category position before The Farmer’s Dog locks it in.
Relevance
High
Builds directly on stated FY26 fresh investment and the existing Buddies platform. No reposition required.
Feasibility
Medium-High
Architecture and brand work are 90-day moves. Operational ramp on fresh is on the company’s timeline, not ours. We do not staff supply chain.
Risks
  • Fresh pet food is operationally complex — cold chain, last-mile, fulfillment. That work belongs inside; we do not own it.
  • Brand permission risk if Blue Buffalo dilutes by chasing The Farmer’s Dog’s ground rather than holding its own.
  • Margin profile of fresh is lower than dry kibble; the membership economics need to hold up when modeled honestly.
Advisory note

Where the advisory layer adds value here: pressure-testing the membership-vs-channel architecture decision before it gets locked in, sparring on disclosure design with IR ahead of launch, and providing an independent read on whether the brand permission strategy is differentiated from The Farmer’s Dog or convergent with it.

Pet operations are not where outside help is needed. But the membership and subscription architecture sitting on top of Pet is genuinely a different problem, and it’s the one structural call where outside pattern recognition is hard to source any other way.
Blue Buffalo has every input The Farmer’s Dog has, plus retail distribution and brand permission they don’t. The architecture question — whether Buddies and fresh are one membership product or two channels — is the kind of decision that gets harder to undo the longer it sits open.
Where Consello earns its keep here is the membership construct, the brand architecture, and the disclosure design with IR. The supply chain and cold-chain work, you have stronger operators on the inside than we’d ever bring to the table.

Lens — Whiteside Confidence 90%

Reframe Good Rewards into a true membership engine.

Why this is second

Highest confidence on the list. Most portable. The membership construct built here scales the Pet logic across the rest of NAR — the largest segment in the business — and gives the company its first disclosable consumer-relationship metric in earnings. Janey did this exact build at Walmart+. The pattern recognition is the value-add.

The strategic frame

Good Rewards launched January 2025 with Fetch. The combined first-party footprint — Good Rewards plus Box Tops plus BettyCrocker.com plus Pillsbury.com — reaches 16 million monthly users with consented household-level data. Cheriti Swigart and the team have publicly framed this as a "marketing engagement platform" (CGT, January 2025). Nature Valley shifted nearly 70% of brand spend to first-party-data-targeted media with high-single-digit lift.

Sixteen million monthly users with consented data is membership scale, not a CRM list. Today the consumer experiences Good Rewards as a Fetch overlay, not a General Mills relationship. The opportunity is to reframe it from a coupon utility into a tiered membership the household sees as worth opening every week. Janey ran exactly this conversion at Walmart+ — taking fragmented perks and unifying them under a single predictable subscription that reduced cognitive load and drove repeat behavior.

What execution looks like
  • Tier architecture: what stays free, what crosses the paywall, where the value exchange feels worth opening the app weekly
  • Fetch relationship redesign: a 6–9 month conversation about what GIS owns directly versus what stays partnered
  • Box Tops integration: clear differentiation by purpose so the two programs don’t cannibalize
  • Cross-portfolio activation: 45+ brands talking to one household through one membership relationship, not 45 fragmented touchpoints
  • Disclosure design: a member metric the CFO can defend in earnings within 12 months
What the board sees in 12 months
  • Active member growth and member household penetration as a stated metric
  • Member-vs-non-member basket size and category cross-purchase lift
  • A retention/repeat metric the CFO can defend in earnings commentary
Impact
High
First disclosable consumer-relationship metric in earnings. Foundation for membership across the portfolio. Multiple expansion lever.
Relevance
High
Direct extension of CGT/Salesforce-cited investments. Builds on the Nature Valley first-party data lift already public.
Feasibility
Medium-High
Fetch partnership creates a 6–9 month restructuring path. Architecture work proceeds in parallel. No technology dependency that blocks start.
Risks
  • The Fetch partnership constrains how much of the experience GIS owns directly. Restructuring is a 6–9 month conversation, not a flip of a switch.
  • Consumer expectations for CPG loyalty programs are low. The bar to feel "remarkable" — Harmening’s word — is correspondingly high.
  • Box Tops cannibalization risk if the two programs aren’t clearly differentiated by purpose.
Advisory note

Where the advisory layer adds value: independent read on whether the Fetch partnership terms are durable enough to support the membership ambition, sparring on tier-design choices that map to actual household behavior, and IR coordination on what to start disclosing and when.

You have 16 million monthly users on your first-party platforms. That’s larger than several public membership businesses. The question we’d start with is whether Good Rewards is being designed as a membership product or as a data-collection layer — because the architecture for those two is different.
Janey architected exactly this conversion at Walmart, taking fragmented perks under a single membership the household opens every week. The construct ports.
The win we’d target by the next investor day: a member metric you can disclose and defend.

Unify the GLP-1, 55+, and Hispanic slate into one brand narrative.

Why this is third

The product slate is already strong — three priority cohorts publicly named, ~25% NPD growth in FY26, elasticities holding on 90% of price investments. The gap is the one-sentence story the Street can repeat. Mindy did this exact reframe at Nike, then ran narrative-led reinventions as the public-company CEO of WW and HSN. The marketing dollars are already being spent — the question is whether they’re in service of one story or three.

The strategic frame

Three priority cohorts were named publicly by leadership at CAGNY (February 2026) and FoodNavigator (October 2025): GLP-1 users, 55+ shoppers, Hispanic households. The innovation pipeline backing those cohorts is real — Cheerios Protein, Ghost partnership, Annie’s Super Mac, Honey Nut Cheerios Protein, smaller pack sizes for Pillsbury.

The vulnerability: the company is communicating these as three separate plays. The Street is reading them as three separate plays. They are actually one play. General Mills is repositioning its core portfolio around the consumer who eats less but more nutritionally dense food — and that consumer is GLP-1 user, 55+ household, and many Hispanic family meal planners simultaneously. Mindy did this exact reframe at Nike with "if you have a body, you are an athlete" — broadening the brand’s permission rather than fragmenting the consumer base.

Where the competitive set is
  • Kraft Heinz: $600M reinvestment, new CEO, halted split. Has a narrative.
  • PepsiCo: Simply NKD line, removing artificial colors from Lay’s and Gatorade. Has a narrative.
  • General Mills: more product news than either, but a less-clear story. Product slate is the asset; narrative is the gap.
What execution looks like
  • Platform brief: the one-sentence story tying all three cohorts together — built around nutritional density and portion intelligence, not weight loss
  • Brand-level activation: coherent advertising platform across Cheerios Protein, Nature Valley, Annie’s, Progresso, Pillsbury small-pack — speaking to one consumer insight
  • Investor-day integration: the platform brief becomes the spine of the next investor day, not a marketing slide buried in the appendix
  • Hispanic authenticity work: real organizational commitment, not a campaign overlay; partnership choices and creative leadership decisions matter
  • Marketing ROI proof: measurably improved efficiency on spend already deployed, since the redirection is of intent, not budget

This is not a cost-cutting brief. It is a marketing-as-investment brief.

Brand-direct first-party media monetization — not a network, a product.

Why this sits last

The most ambitious recommendation and the least signaled by leadership. Leading with this in the first conversation overweights what is in fact a horizon idea, when there are more grounded moves to make first. Hold for the second meeting unless the client raises it directly.

The strategic frame

This is the recommendation that requires the most care in framing. General Mills is not Walmart and should not pretend it can build Walmart Connect (which did $4.4B in FY25 ad revenue per Marketing Dive coverage). What it can do — and what analyst commentary has not fully surfaced — is monetize its 16M monthly first-party audience as a brand-direct media product for adjacent, non-competing partners: kitchen appliance OEMs, complementary grocery, family-finance services, GLP-1 adjacent health brands.

The proof point is in their own commentary. The Pillsbury / air-fryer targeting case Mark Nudi described publicly shows General Mills already has the audience-building capability internally. The gap is that today it is used only to drive their own brand sales. A modest commercial layer would be the highest-margin dollar in the company.

Estimate — state as such

Year-one revenue range: $30–75M [estimate, sized against retail-media adjacency economics where mature CPG-adjacent networks run at 70%+ contribution margins].

Specific GIS sizing requires audience-engagement data that is not in public filings. Do not commit to a number in the room — commit to a scoping conversation that produces one.

What execution would look like
  • Anchor partner identification: 3–5 non-competing brands where audience match is provable
  • Product design: a brand-direct media product, not a network — selling targeted audience, not inventory
  • Channel-conflict management: structured to be complement to retailer media networks (Walmart, Kroger), not substitute
  • Privacy and consent architecture: consented household data, GIS-controlled, defensible to legal review
  • FY27 stated revenue line: a soft-launched product disclosed in the FY27 outlook

The advisory layer — cutting through the noise.

Distinct from areas 1–5

Areas 1–5 are execution offers. This is different. The advisory layer is a continuous operator-level thought partnership that sits across the entire portfolio — including the parts of the business we are not staffing directly, including the parts that are working well, and including the parts where the right move is to do less rather than more. It is the layer designed to help cut through the noise of any operating role and add clarity about where to lean in and where to pull back.

What this is

An ongoing relationship with operators who have done this work — Mindy as a sitting public-company CEO twice over, Janey as the architect of two consumer platforms inside Walmart, Wendy as the operator of a global agency network. Not consultants. Not retainers. People who have made the equivalent decisions and now act as an independent expert voice for someone navigating their own version of the same questions.

This layer runs alongside execution work but does not require it. It can be the only thing the engagement is. It can be the thing that opens the door to execution work later. Or it can be how the relationship begins, with the rest deferred to whether the right work surfaces.

What it looks like in practice
  • Monthly operator-level sessions: a working forum with Mindy and Janey, structured to surface what isn’t being said in the operating cadence — what to lean into, what to pull back from, where the noise is loudest, where the signal is being missed
  • Pre-meeting prep: independent inputs ahead of board sessions, investor days, earnings calls — a second read on the narrative, the disclosure, the Q&A risk surface
  • Strategic decision pressure-testing: when a structural call is being weighed (org design, portfolio shape, capital allocation), an outside vantage point that has lived through the equivalent at scale
  • Independent voice on tradeoffs: the conversations where the answer requires saying no to something the organization wants to say yes to — where the value of an outside operator is precisely that they don’t carry the internal politics
  • Pattern recognition across our portfolio: we sit alongside other operators in adjacent industries and bring back what we’re seeing, anonymously and selectively, when it’s useful
Where it adds the most
  • Onboarding into a new role: the first 6–12 months of any expanded scope is when the highest-leverage decisions get made and the operating cadence is least settled. Outside perspective compounds.
  • Pressure-testing objectives: separating urgent from important, making sure the wins being prioritized are the wins that actually matter
  • Cutting through the noise: the operating role takes inputs from dozens of sources — management team, board, Street, agencies, consultants, peers. The advisory layer is one independent voice whose only job is to be useful to the operator
  • Coverage of the working parts of the business: the parts that don’t need execution help still need a sounding board on whether they’re truly working or just performing well
How it’s structured
  • Senior partner time: Mindy primary, Janey and Wendy in support
  • Monthly cadence baseline; on-call for high-stakes moments
  • Modest, predictable retainer — not staffed engagement economics
  • Contractually independent from execution workstreams; no incentive to recommend more work
  • Structured to be the relationship that lasts, not the project that ends

06Sequencing

The order is deliberate. Pet (Area 1) leads because the architecture window in pet humanization closes fastest and the membership/subscription decision is uniquely Consello-shaped — operations stay inside, the architectural call is where outside pattern recognition adds the most. Good Rewards (Area 2) is highest-confidence and scales the same membership logic across the largest segment in the company. The unified narrative (Area 3) is the multiple re-rate — Mindy’s reinvention lens applied to a product slate that’s already strong. The operating-model reset (Area 4) is the funding mechanism that pays for areas 1–3 without an incremental ask. First-party media (Area 5) is the horizon idea, held back unless the door opens from inside.

None of areas 1–5 require IT infrastructure work, supply-chain rebuilds, or legal restructuring. All sit inside the Consello Brand, Experience & Growth staffing footprint and could be in flight within 30 days of engagement.

Area 6 — the advisory layer — is structurally different. It is not a workstream. It runs continuously across the engagement, including across the parts of the portfolio not being staffed directly. It can stand alone, run alongside execution, or be how the relationship begins.

The single most important sequencing call

Stand up Area 4 — operating-model and marketing efficiency review — in parallel with Areas 1 and 2 in the first 60 days. Area 4 funds Areas 1, 2, and 3 from reallocated spend rather than incremental budget asks. That is the difference between a 12-month plan that lands at the next investor day and one that gets stuck in the budget cycle.
