Part 01 — Integrated analysis

The 24-month picture

AG1 spent fifteen years as the textbook single-SKU, DTC, subscription business. In the last two years it deliberately broke every one of those constraints at once — new CEO, reformulation, flavors, a second and third product, and a rush into mass retail — while quietly putting itself up for sale. The former-leader account fills in the why it's straining underneath that expansion.

The strategic inflection

The throughline is a single pivot: from "one product, one channel, one flavor, DTC" to a diversified portfolio across mass retail. CEO Kat Cole (who succeeded founder Chris Ashenden in July 2024, after he resigned amid disclosures about pre-AG1 legal issues) framed it at NRF 2025 as the company finally "saying yes" after years of saying no. The decisive context: in April 2026, Reuters reported (via Fast Company) AG1 was shopping itself at a $2B+ valuation, and Cole told Modern Retail that channel and product diversification "makes us more attractive" for an exit. The land-grab and the sale process are the same story.

Former leader feedbackCorroborated

The product focus reads short-term — chasing vanity metrics over membership health — which is rational if the goal is investment or sale. The pattern looks like "throwing things at the wall": diverting from the hero into too many individual products, then launching new formula, new flavors, and sleep all at once.

The sale process and the compressed launch cadence are confirmed publicly. The "vanity over membership health" characterization is the insider's read, not a published metric.

Product evolution

  • AG1 Next Gen (Jan 2025) — biggest reformulation in years: serving 12g→13g, probiotics ~7.2B→10B CFU, more clinical backing. Solubility improved; taste shifted.
  • New flavors (mid-2025) — Tropical, Citrus, Berry added to Original. AG1 itself concedes a majority still prefer Original.
  • AGZ (Aug 2025) — first all-new product in years; melatonin-free nighttime "rest & restore" powder. The "day-to-night" stacking story.
  • AG1 Pro (June 2026) — Next Gen plus 5g creatine, Ca-HMB, zinc carnosine; aimed at performance/longevity and GLP-1 users. Cole noted 30%+ of customers already stack creatine.
Former leader feedbackPartly corroborated

The Pro coherence problem. Pro is the "elite" tier, but it started as an elite-athlete product and broadened to everyone. That undercuts the brand's long-standing claim that the core is the best — which no longer holds if AG1 keeps adding "needed" products alongside the hero rather than building on top of it. Pro performs well, but artificially: its forecast was cut back sharply from the original plan.

Pro's elite-to-everyone positioning and the hero-coherence tension are visible in the launch framing. The forecast cut and "artificial" performance are internal-only.

Former leader feedbackCorroborated

Flavors as a retention tool that wasn't. New flavors were positioned as the answer; they helped acquire new members but did not win back lapsed ones — because too few sampling opportunities were created to put new flavors in at-risk and lapsed hands.

Flavor launch and the taste-change backlash are corroborated; the acquire-not-reactivate split and under-resourced sampling are the insider's read, consistent with the public taste complaints.

The stacking paradox

AG1's founding promise was the end of the supplement stack: one scoop replaces the cabinet of bottles. The current shop sells the opposite. Alongside Next Gen sit Pro, AGZ, Omega3, Omega3 + CoQ10, and Vitamin D3+K2 — and AG1's own merchandising bundles them into a set it literally calls the "Full Foundation Stack." The brand built to kill the stack now names one after itself.

Each add-on is a quiet admission that "one scoop covers it" was never fully true — D3+K2 and Omega3 exist precisely because they were left out of the flagship. Defensible on the science; corrosive to the promise. And the sprawl has no organizing logic because the core customer was never defined. "Foundational nutrition for everyone" gives no principled basis for what to add, or for whom — so the shelf just accumulates, the product finder can't differentiate, and the SKUs cannibalize one another. An ill-defined customer produces an unbounded product line.

Why this is bigger than a merchandising note

Simplicity was the moat. "You don't need twelve bottles, just take AG1" is the entire reason the brand could charge $79/month in a commodity category. Every add-on trades a little of that equity for incremental order value — and to a buyer, a six-SKU stack sold by a one-scoop brand reads as the flagship's growth stalling and the company reaching for revenue wherever it can find it.

The three cancel drivers

Public reviews independently surface all three forces below — taste volatility, value (not price) doubt, and subscription/over-supply friction. The former leader's contribution is the ranking and the mechanics, which only an insider would hold.

Former leader feedback — Cancel driver #1Corroborated

Overstock. The original product shipped in a refrigerated pouch + canister. Next Gen introduced travel-friendly packaging; customers are shifting to it, but Original remains the most-subscribed format — and the newer pack makes it easier to extend product life, so supply outpaces consumption and members become over-supplied. Subscriptions run on Skio (a Shopify-native subscription platform): great for self-service, but it also makes pausing and cancelling frictionless.

AG1 publicly concedes most customers prefer Original, and confirms the pouch/canister-vs-travel split. The easy-cancel self-service dynamic shows up in reviews. The overstock-as-#1-cancel-reason ranking and AG1's use of Skio specifically are internal-only.

Former leader feedback — Cancel driver #2Corroborated

Taste. Taste is a leading reason to leave, and the reformulation hit the loyalists for whom flavor was the reason to stay.

2025–26 reviews report a bitter/metallic Next Gen aftertaste and loyalists noting the taste gap to cheaper competitors narrowed. The exact cancel-reason ranking is internal.

Former leader feedback — Cancel driver #3Partly corroborated

Cost — really value. The lever has been gift-with-purchase and discounting, not added value. There are no real loyalty mechanics (e.g., streaks) to reinforce behavior; free product just worsens overstock; and there's little targeting or segmentation to manage against.

Reviews consistently frame the issue as value ("replicate it for less"), and discount/GWP reliance is visible everywhere. AG1's "Membership" is a subscription-with-perks, not gamified loyalty — consistent with "no streaks." The internal specifics on segmentation aren't public.

What's working vs. what isn't

Holding up

  • The loyal core. Decade-long subscribers and a durable Original base remain the real asset.
  • Profitability & self-funding. Cole says profitable for years, funding its own innovation and expansion.
  • Trust scaffolding. NSF Certified for Sport, $20M clinical commitment (2025–28).
  • Retail as trial funnel. Lower-commitment packs address the "won't pay $79/mo unseen" barrier.
  • GLP-1 timing. Pro's muscle-preservation angle is well-aimed.

Straining

  • Reformulation backlash. Next Gen's taste change hit a key reason loyalists stayed.
  • Value perception. Persistent "replicate it for less" sentiment — a value, not price, problem.
  • Brand coherence. If Next Gen is "the best," why Pro, AGZ, and four flavors?
  • The gummy threat. Grüns/Unilever owns the format AG1 hasn't cracked.
  • Culture signals. Layoffs, ambiguous bonuses, exec turnover; recommend rate ~70%→~48%.

Part 03 — Executional read

The pattern, and what it costs in a sale

Strip away the individual moves and one pattern remains: velocity without orchestration. AG1 shipped a decade of change in eighteen months and treated each initiative as a discrete bet rather than a sequenced system. Motion is being read as progress.

Three ways it shows up

  • Timing. Everything launched at once. Costco hit the shelf in the same window as the reformulation — old formula, lower price, no connecting story — and flavors, AGZ, and Pro stacked on top of each other before any one proved out.
  • Consequences. Second-order effects on the base were absorbed after the fact, not designed around: the reformulation changed the taste loyalists stayed for; the Lululemon bag bought deal-seekers with no retention hook; Pro launched without resolving what it does to "the core is the best."
  • Measurement. Tests weren't built to give clean answers — the Function Health trial confounded AG1's effect with Function's, and the headline retention number appears propped up by the year-one cohort. Fast, uninstrumented launches produce noise, not signal.

None of this is random. It's what a business optimizing for a near-term sale looks like: the clock rewards a visible growth story — "multi-product, omnichannel platform now" — over durable results, because diversification is easier to show a buyer than retention discipline is to build.

What it means in diligence

The trap: the activity meant to raise the price is exactly what diligence punishes. A buyer converts a clean story into a scored one.

  • The crown jewel gets tested first. AG1 is sold on loyalty. DD de-averages the cohorts on day one — if the year-one base carries the number, the real churn curve is discovered by the buyer instead of disclosed by the seller. Worst possible way for it to surface, and it comes straight off the multiple.
  • Revenue quality is questioned. Discount/GWP acquisition, frictionless cancel, and overstock-driven churn all mark lower-quality recurring revenue. Buyers pay up for durable, full-margin revenue and discount promotional, leaky revenue.
  • Nothing attributes cleanly. Because everything moved at once and the tests were confounded, management can't answer "what's driving growth?" with clean data — and buyers meet that with conservative assumptions, wider earnouts, bigger holdbacks.
  • New risks were added, not retired. Channel price-integrity (Costco), brand coherence against a now-scaled Grüns/Unilever, product sprawl, and org instability are all fresh flags a disciplined single-SKU business never carried.
The irony, stated plainly

A focused, profitable, high-margin single-SKU business with a loyal core is an easier asset to underwrite than a half-executed diversification. Clean-and-proven commands a premium; messy-and-unproven gets discounted. The expansion needed to be either far enough along to prove out, or not started — caught in the middle is the most expensive place to be sold. Unless AG1 walks into DD able to show the new bets working with clean attribution, the rush to build the story may have lowered the quality of the story.

Part 04 — Synthesis

Where the biggest opportunity sits

Outside analysis and the insider account converge on one diagnosis: AG1 built a category-defining loyal core, then spent two years adding surface area faster than it built the membership architecture to hold it. The opportunity is almost entirely on the retention/value side — the part the expansion playbook neglected.

01

Re-architect the line as base + stack, not parallel products

Next Gen as the protected hero; AGZ, Pro, and future SKUs as need-state add-ons that build on it, gated to a defined customer — not a shelf of competing "bests." AG1 already sells a "Full Foundation Stack"; the job is to give that stack a logic. Fixes "unclear what to buy," cannibalization, and the dead product-finder — and re-legitimizes "the core is the best."

Originated in former leader feedback
02

Move from discount/GWP to genuine value and loyalty mechanics

The cancel driver is value, not price — yet the defense has been free product (worsens overstock) and bags (buys deal-seekers). Replace with behavioral loyalty (streaks, tenure benefits, milestone unlocks), consumption-paced fulfillment to kill the overstock cancel, and proof-of-value moments. The cleanest fit for a membership-economics engagement.

03

Fix segmentation and the cohort-blind metrics

If aggregate retention is propped up by the year-one cohort, the dashboard hides the real churn curve. De-average it, build at-risk/lapsed segments, and instrument the sampling-for-retention play the account says never got resourced.

Supported by former leader feedback
04

Make retail a DTC funnel, not a discount leak

The Target trial kit is the right instinct; the Costco old-formula-at-a-lower-price episode is the cautionary tale. Define the channel role (trial → DTC membership), fix on-pack value comms, and protect DTC value perception so retail builds LTV instead of training the base to wait for the cheaper shelf.

05

Close the format/occasion gap before it's an exit liability

Grüns just proved the gummy/kids/convenience format is worth ~$1.2B to Unilever. AG1 has the brand and the science but not the form factor. Whether the goal is defense or a higher sale multiple, owning a credible convenient/kids format — and the GLP-1 occasion Pro already chases — is where category share is moving.

The Consello angle, unsaid

Four of these five are loyalty, membership-economics, CRM, and lifecycle problems — the exact operator-credible work the BEG practice sells. If AG1 is genuinely in a sale process, a sharper retention story and a coherent portfolio architecture are also the two levers that most move the multiple. That's the wedge.
