Deliverable 01 · Narrative

How we talk about it

For anyone putting words on a page. Answers: how do I say it.
This is a framework, not a script.
No one walks up to a customer and says "welcome to M&T, our vision is to be the bank invited into homes." The statements are not lines to recite. They set the direction, and they align what we say, how we say it, and most of all how we live it. Used right, you rarely quote them. You hear them in how everything else is written.

The work in this guide is learning to write and speak so the direction comes through, without ever reading the direction out loud.

The engine behind every reframe

Customer-centricity is the move, not the message

We are already customer-centric in what we do. The gap is that we describe it from the bank's side. The whole craft here is one shift: make the customer the subject of the sentence.

What we tend to write

The bank built a capability. The bank invested. The bank strengthened its operations. True, but the customer is offstage, and the reader has to work out why it matters to them.

What customer-centric writing does

The customer gets something they can feel. The same fact, told so the person on the other side is the one it happens to. Nothing about the work changes. What changes is who the sentence is about.

The test you can run on any sentence: if you took M&T out as the hero, is there still a customer who is better off? If not, rewrite it until there is. That single habit is what the reframes at the end of this section all share.

How to use it · Purpose

The Purpose: the "why," in your people's words

To make a difference in people's lives
by knowing them, growing with them, and connecting them with everything they need to thrive.

The core line never moves. The "by" clause is where your part of the bank makes it real, keeping the three verbs, knowing, growing, connecting, and pointing them at the people you actually serve.

How to use it · Vision

The Vision: the bank customers choose to let in

To be the bank invited into homes, businesses, and communities
by earning trust and deepening relationships with every interaction.

The Vision is the one place "invited" belongs. It names the outcome from the customer's side: being asked further in because we earned it. You almost never say the Vision out loud. You use it to check whether what you are writing points at that outcome.

When you reach for it

When you describe where we are headed and want the customer, not the bank, doing the choosing. Brand work, aspiration, the future state.

How it shapes the writing

If a line brags about what M&T did, the Vision pulls it back to what the customer felt that made them want more. Earned, then invited, never the other way round.

The tell that you are using the Vision well: the customer is inviting M&T in, not M&T announcing it arrived. "A bank people ask into their biggest decisions" is Vision. "The bank expanding into new markets" is not.

The guardrails

Consistent without being rigid

Lead with the spirit, hold the few keywords fixed, flex everything else. A banker in Buffalo and an advisor in Wilmington still describe M&T the same way.

Stays fixed

  • Customer first. Their need leads; the stance is active and earned.
  • The core Purpose. "Make a difference in people's lives" is not redrafted.
  • Earn, and invited. "Earn" is the working verb everywhere; "invited" lives only in the Vision.

Can flex

  • Tone. Sound like yourself. The anchor is the direction, not the wording.
  • Length. Short on a poster, full in a formal piece.
  • Voice. First, third, or active, by audience.
  • The "by" clause. Adapt it to your area, keep the three verbs.

Avoid

  • Function ahead of the customer. Tech and process are framed by what they do for the client.
  • Reciting the North Star as a slogan. It is a direction, not a tagline.
  • "Community bank." We are a bank for communities. The difference matters.

Reframes in practice

We do not tear things down. We read them through the customer.

The point of what follows is not the specific wording. It is the move. Take something we already wrote, keep the words and the intent, and add or shift just enough that the customer is the one it happens to. Do that across our existing artifacts and the direction becomes real without anything being rebuilt.

Three examples follow, on three kinds of writing: a values list, a shareholder letter, and our internal ways of working. Same move every time.

Reframe 1 of 3 · A values list

The values stay. One line each turns them outward.

TODAY · OWNERSHIP
We understand what is important to our customers and communities. We execute with urgency. We follow through on commitments. We act responsibly to preserve our future.
READ THROUGH THE CUSTOMER
We understand what is important to our customers and communities. We execute with urgency. We follow through on commitments. We act responsibly to preserve our future. We take personal responsibility to create better outcomes for those we serve, not just for the transactions we complete.
What changed

Nothing was removed. One sentence was added that names the customer as the point of the value.

Why

The value was inward, about how we conduct ourselves. The addition makes it externally felt, about what the customer gets.

The logic to reuse

For any internal statement, ask what it produces for a customer, and add that as the last line. Keep the original intact.

Reframe 2 of 3 · Long-form prose

Same investment. Told as a promise to the customer.

TODAY
We made a deliberate investment in Operational Excellence as a shared framework for running the company. We see this as stewardship of the franchise, building the capacity to grow responsibly while preserving the discipline and culture that define M&T.
REFRAME
Our customers were telling us something: their experience did not always match what our people were capable of delivering. The relationships were strong; the systems behind them created friction. We invested in Operational Excellence to make every customer interaction faster, simpler, and more worthy of the trust our customers place in us.
What changed

The subject moved from the bank stewarding itself to the customer feeling the difference. Same investment, same outcome.

Why

"Stewardship of the franchise" is the bank admiring its own discipline. The customer is nowhere in it.

The logic to reuse

When prose explains a decision, lead with what the customer was experiencing that made it necessary, then what they feel now.

Reframe 3 of 3 · Internal ways of working

The mindset holds. The customer becomes the test.

TODAY · FOCUS ON WHAT MATTERS
We prioritize impact, deliver value incrementally, and measure success.
REFRAME
What matters is whether the client's life is better because they bank with us. We prioritize impact, deliver value incrementally, and measure success against that.
What changed

The mindset words are all still there. They now sit under a customer test instead of standing as their own goal.

Why

"Measure success" begs the question: success at what? The reframe answers it with the customer.

The logic to reuse

When a statement describes how we work, put the customer outcome first as the thing the work is measured against.

That is the whole method. Across a values list, a letter, and a way of working, the same single move. Now you can run it on anything we have already written.

Deliverable 02 · Strategy

Where it fits in the work

For anyone deciding what to back, what to drop, and what to say about it. Answers: where does this sit.
The same words can mean who we are or what we are doing this quarter. Those are different jobs.
When a leader reaches for the language, it helps to know whether they are naming why the bank exists, where it is heading, or the work in front of a team on Tuesday. All of it answers to the same customer. That is what keeps the pieces connected, and it is the thing this guide makes plain.

It is the bridge between how we talk 01 · Narrative and how we lead the work 03 · Leadership.

The test that does the work

Can you draw the line to the customer?

Trace it both ways. Any plan or project should trace a clean line back to the North Star, and through it to a customer who is better off. If you cannot draw that line, the work needs reframing, or it needs to stop. And in reverse: do not use "who we are" to win a fight that belongs to "what we are doing now." The customer is the test at every level.

This is not a new process or a checklist on top of the work. It is permission to ask a question we sometimes skip: does this actually earn more of the relationship, deeper, longer, or wider? When the answer is no, the decision changes.

It does not replace what we already run

It anchors the priorities we already have

Teaming for Growth and Operational Excellence are the engines. The North Star points them at the same place and makes each easier to run.

Teaming for Growth

Cross-business work stops being about the org chart and becomes about one question: did the client get the whole bank? No customer is limited by the relationship they happened to start with.

Operational Excellence

Earning the right to the relationship becomes the goal the work serves. Customer-back processes, faster service, fewer handoffs, measured by whether it was easier for the customer and the people serving them.

What the North Star produces when it anchors both: one destination instead of two parallel tracks, a customer outcome attached to every measure of success, and a shared test the room can use when the two engines pull against each other. Operational Excellence in service of Teaming for Growth, not running beside it.

Deliverable 03 · Leadership

Bringing people with us

For a manager about to roll something out. Answers: what do I do.
A new capability only changes the bank when the people running it can see themselves in it.
As we build in technology, wealth, data, and customer experience, the direction is how each person understands why we are doing this and where their own work fits. Drawing that line is not a communications task handed off to someone else. It is part of leading.

When the line gets drawn, people build the change with us. When it does not, they experience it as something happening to them.

©2026 M&T Bank · Internal use onlyLeadership
The standard we hold ourselves to

Make the path visible

When leaders draw the line clearly and often, people understand the direction and their place in it. When the line goes undrawn, people fill the gap themselves, and it fills with uncertainty. Everyone who shows up, adapts, and deepens what they bring earns a place in the next chapter of this bank. A leader's job is to make that path visible.

This is not about getting people to recite the direction. It is about leading them from where they are today to where the bank is going, one connection at a time, so they help build it rather than brace against it.

What every leader answers for their team

Three questions, answered before they are asked

Change explained only as strategy or a new tool leaves people asking what it means for them. When you do not answer, they guess. Answer these in order.

1

What is this, and why are we doing it?

Name it plainly and tie it to how we earn the relationship. We are doing this so we move together, decide faster, hand off less, and serve people better, which is how we earn the right to stay.

2

Where does my work fit?

Draw the line from the priority to the role. Every function connects to the same direction once a leader makes the connection out loud.

3

What is being asked of me, and what backs me up?

Be clear about how the direction sharpens their focus, and what they get to make it real: clearer goals, the language to use, the support behind it.

The shorthand: what is it, where do I fit, what is asked of me. If a person can answer all three in their own words after you talk, you led the change. If they cannot, you announced it.

How it sounds in a real rollout

One sentence turns an announcement into a reason

The same move from the narrative guide works inside the bank. Add the line that connects the work to the customer.

WHAT WE USUALLY SEND
"We are rolling out the new CRM platform in Q3. Training materials will be in the learning portal."
WITH THE LINE DRAWN
"We are rolling out the new CRM platform in Q3. This is part of how we earn the relationship. When the system knows what a customer needs before they have to repeat themselves, trust deepens. Training materials will be in the learning portal."
What changed

One sentence. The facts and the logistics are untouched.

Why it works

People now see why the change matters and what it asks of them, so they join it instead of absorbing it.

The logic to reuse

Before any rollout note goes out, add the sentence that says what this earns for the customer.

Before you roll anything out

Five questions to run first

If you cannot answer yes to each, the capability may be ready but the leadership around it is not.

  • Have I tied this to where the bank is going? Before I say what it does, have I said why it matters?
  • Can every person affected see their own place in it? Not the team in general. Each role.
  • Have I said what changes for them, not just what changes? The difference between a feature list and a reason to care is the second person.
  • Have I connected it to the customer? Can my team say how this earns our way in or the right to stay?
  • Have I planned the first win? Do I know the early result I will mark, and who I will credit for it?
